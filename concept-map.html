How Ono's translation register reveals a layer of unreliable narration

Ishiguro's use of a translation register allows for the formality of Japanese language to exhibit through Ono's narration even in English.

Ono recounts his past events through social language, and Japanese social language is full of hedging, deference, and circumlocution, and it is one of the most well-documented features of Japanese sociolinguistics.

Ishiguro is able to bake these aspects of language into Ono's english narration. When Ono says things such as "it is possible that..." or "I have reason to believe that on that occasion" or softening every claim with qualifiers, his narration appears natural culturally. This also means that Ono does not ever have to make a direct statement about his past actions, as the conventions of politeness weaved within the language allows Ono to hide between the lines of cultural formality and evasion, producing ambiguity and drives the novel's unreliable narration.

Ono narrates negative events, such as his role in the arrest of Kuroda, watching the collapse of his wartime influence, and seeing his daughter's marriage prospects and lives damaged by his reputation. However, he narrates these events in the same formal voice he uses to recount positive events, such as an evening at a bar with his students and colleagues. The flatness and absence of emotion is a form of unreliable narration as Ono's voice refuses to acknowledge the emotion that should show in the language when recounting some of the more traumatic memories, suppressing their emotional weight.

Ishiguro's personal fear

Source: https://www.theguardian.com/books/2016/jun/24/kazuo-ishiguro-my-turning-point-reading-proust-on-my-sickbed

In the article, Ishiguro names his own personal fear, “And for me personally: the nagging sense of how difficult it is to see clearly above the dogmatic fervours of one’s day; and the fear that time and history would show that for all one’s good intentions, one had backed a wrong, shameful, even evil cause, and wasted one’s best years and talents to it.”

This statement is a close description of Ono's own life. Ono devoted his best years and his real talent as an artist to a cause that he believed was right and noble, which was his nationalist projects. However, history reveals it as shameful. Through a projection of Ono, Ishiguro is confessing that Ono's issues is his own fear, and Ono represents a construct that has been built as if his fear had already come true. This tells the audience that Ishiguro did not write about Ono for Ono to be condemned and shamed, he wrote him with a fear and sympathy, as Ono is who Ishiguro was afraid of becoming, and this reveals the purpose of the unreliable narration and self deception of Ono.

Rather than unreliable narration and self deception being a process of villainy and deception, it is simply a human process of a person unable to face the thought that they gave their life to something that was now deemed shameful, and the evasions, drifting memories, and reframing are like psychological coping mechanisms of someone who's entire self esteem and sense of self worth depends on NOT seeing themselves clearly. The unreliability becomes tragic rather than deceptive. Instead of watching a liar attempting to hide his actions, the narrative presents a story of a man's mind protecting him from a truth destroy him. The audience is able to see what Ono's mind is rejecting and cannot bear seeing, resulting in the audience feeling pity, not satisfaction in Ono's sufferings.

Ono is essentially a mirror to the audience about the fears that Ishiguro puts forward and makes the audience think, how would you know, in your own political moment and thoughts, whether or not you are an 'Ono', and how is anyone able to see clearly, as Ishiguro puts it "above the dogmatic fervours of one’s day".

How the structure of Ono's narration enacts self-deception

Ono's narrative structure differs from conventionally structured novels, where a narrator could tell the audience of their self-deception, explicitly depicting their denial. Ono utilises his inspiration of Proust's work and makes the structure of narration itself reveal the deception.

Whenever Ono remembers something painful or negative in his past, such as his role in Kuroda's arrest, his responsibility for the failed marriage negotiations etc, the memory always shifts to a different time period with a different event. Ono is not denying the truth or refusing to talk about the event, he is coincidently just remembering something else.

The structural movement mimics psychological avoidance, which is when the mind uses protective behaviours to redirect itself before it faces its fears. It provides immediate emotional relief, but in the long term it reinforces fears and maintains anxiety disorders.

Ishiguro also places scenes next to one another and lets the audience map out the relationship between them, as stated in How the structure of Ono's narration betrays his story. The disarray between what the structure is telling the audience and what the narrator claims reveals the self-deception of Ono.

How the structure of Ono's narration enacts self-deception

How the structure of Ono's narration betrays his story

In An Artist of the Floating World, Ishiguro writes in a non-linear narration structure through writing scenes in order of association (See The Proustian influence on the narrative structure of "An Artist of the Floating World").

In the October 1948 section where Ono is narrating the event of discussions around Noriko's upcoming miai with the Saito family and about his unease involving the previous failed negotiations with the Miyake family. Mid narration, his flow of memory turns the event being narrated into his days studying under Mori-san, his break from his sensei's aestheticism and being An Artist of the Floating World, his turn towards propaganda art, and his rising influence in wartime cultural circles.

Ono presents these as fond memories, such as "let me tell you about my career." However, it makes the reader ponder about why Ono, who was in the middle of worrying about his daughter's marriage prospects, is now recounting the story of his former wartime glory to the audience.

This structure reveals how Ono's flow of memory is betraying him to the reader. Although Ono explicitly states that the Miyake's family withdrawal from marriage negotiations was unrelated to his reputation during the war, his 'flow of memory' style of narration reveals how the two memories are connected, and hence the audience is able to see that the Miyake's negotiations fell apart due to Ono's wartime reputation. Ono never states this explicitly but the proximity of his recounts events exposes the truth.

The effect of this on the audience is the intimacy of this style of narration transforming into an unease as we realise Ono's voice is concealing the truth.

The Proustian influence on the narrative structure of "An Artist of the Floating World"

Source: https://www.theguardian.com/books/2016/jun/24/kazuo-ishiguro-my-turning-point-reading-proust-on-my-sickbed

Ishiguro credits reading the "Overture" and "Combray" sections of Proust's Remembrance of Things for non-linear narration and his novel's form.

Ishiguro adopted from Proust what he called "methods of movement", where scenes are ordered not by chronology or linear plot, but by thought association and the vagaries of memory (unpredictable, erratic and uncontrollable nature of human recollection). This is why Ono's narration is non-linear, where a scene from two days ago is placed next to one from twenty years ago, resulting in the reader being asked to infer the relationship.

An example of this is in the October 1948 section where Ono is narrating the event of discussions around Noriko's upcoming miai with the Saito family and about his unease involving the previous failed negotiations with the Miyake family. Mid narration, his flow of memory turns the event being narrated into his days studying under Mori-san, his break from his sensei's aestheticism and being An Artist of the Floating World, his turn towards propaganda art, and his rising influence in wartime cultural circles.

Ono removes the personalisation of his problem and wants to generalise his own guilts into something like a universal law of human nature through use of language such as "a man" and "the nature of things". Since Ono also states the standard himself clearly, but we cannot dismiss him as stupid or evil for that, making the pity and sympathy stronger.

Rejection of "Prose Screenplay"

Source: https://www.theguardian.com/books/2016/jun/24/kazuo-ishiguro-my-turning-point-reading-proust-on-my-sickbed

Ishiguro was reading his own first novel next to the screenplays he was writing for Channel 4, which resulted in "a significant, if contrary impact of the writing of An Artist". Ishiguro noticed his fiction looked like a line of dialogue from the screenplays, which was a bit of description of what someone did, followed by lines of dialogue. Ishiguro referred to it as "prose screenplay", his fear being that if a novel is just describing observable events and dialogue, then it is not offering anything more than what you could get from watching a TV show, so as Ishiguro states, "Why bother to write a novel if it was going to offer more or less the same experience someone could have by turning on a television?"

Ishiguro solves this through the use of interiority, or living inside a mind (Ono's) with all its distortions about the truth and lies. If An Artist of the Floating World was to be filmed, a camera would reveal and show Ono reporting his student, Kuroda, to the authorities, and the audience would be able to see the consequences on Kuroda's face. They would have the objective truth about the event, and Ono's attempts to minimise and redirect away from it would be easily exposed as lies.

However, Ishiguro's rejection of "prose screenplay" resulted in an interiority where there is no resulting objective footage and only Ono's unreliable accounts. Ishiguro locks the audience inside a single mind with no way to verify its thoughts against reality, which is only possible in prose. By writing against screenplay, Ishiguro creates a story where the only reality is Ono's own constructs. Without this structural choice, the unreliability of Ono fails as the readers can see through an objective lense.

This results in the reader not finishing the book and judging Ono, but rather wondering whether or not they can recognise the same issues Ono had inside them, as Ono could not despite his talent.

Themes and symbols of An Artist of the Floating World

The Floating World (transcience & self-deception)

The Floating World (ukiyo) is a pre-war pleasure district depicted often in woodblock prints which show the pleasure seeking life of Edo period Japan of bars and nocturnal beauty. By definition it is transient, put together at night and vanish with the morning.

The Floating World works as a symbol of Ono's transience as his prestige and moral certainties all vanished like the pleasure district itself, and as a tool for self deception as Ono mourns a vanished world, shown through his muses on rebuilding the pleasure district and reminiscing on his days in the bars with his colleagues and students, however Ono himself helped destroy this world without him seeing his involvement or connection.

Ishiguro wrote An Artist of the Floating World during a politically difficult time in Britain during the 1980s when it was the era of the miner's strike, the Wapping dispute, CND marches, the Falkland war, IRA terrorism, the economic theory of "Monetarism", etc. Ishiguro states he was living in a place where 'the pressures on people in every walk of life to take political sides; the rigid certainties, shading into self-righteousness and sinister aggression, of ardent, often youthful factions; the agonising about the “role of the artist” in a time of political change.'

Ishiguro states that the novel was very much shaped by the Britain in which Ishiguro was in, and the pressures on the people living in Britain to take political sides translates directly onto Ono in the context of wartime Japan.

Ono succumbed to the pressure to take a political side by leaving behind the apolitical aestheticism of the floating world of pleasure and beauty of his teacher Mori-san in order to make nationalist propaganda art, solidifying that he had picked a side.

Ono did not only hold nationalist views, he also enforced them, shown by when he reported his own student, Kuroda to the authorities for being unpatriotic, which resulted in the arrest and torturing of Kuroda, revealing the 'sinister aggression of ardent factions' and making it personal and up close.

By setting it in postwar Japan, Ishiguro can explore his own anxieties in 1980s about Britain at a safe distance, he is not writing about Thatcher, but using an already passed historical event, being the militarism of Japan and its defeat, in order to think about what it feels like to be inside a political moment, where you also cannot see who is right achieved through the interiority of Ono.

Generational change & the East-West paradigm shift

The novel is staged in a nation that is reforming itself from the old order of Bushido, Edo-era honour codes and imperial nationalism to a westernised, democratic, commercial postwar Japan, and this embodied in the two generations in the novel. His daughters, Setsuko and Noriko and his grandson represent the new Japan while Ono is still clinging onto the old world and values.

An example is Ichiro, Ono's grandson, idolising cowboys over samurai heroes, where Ichiro's imagination has already been colonised by the West, and Ono's discomfort shows that he clings to the idea that Japanese heroes should inspire the boy with the same train of thought that once drove the motivation behind his nationalist art, revealing he has not yet moved on. This small detail also shows the entire extent of the paradigm shift, how the next generation won't even inherit the values Ono gave his entire life to. The audience sees the old world ending through a child's harmless playing with his cowboy western idol, which makes it feel inevitable rather than a contested possibility.

Another example is Setsuko's repeated warnings to Ono about the marriage negotiations and how he may need to take "precautionary steps". Setsuko does not attack Ono's reputation directly as the new world is too polite for that, however she pressures him to disown it for their family's survival. This interaction shows the generational change, where the child now instructs the parents and the values that Ono once honoured are now a liability, revealing how the postwar order totally changed and overturned the old hierarchy.

Ishiguro asks the audience a question through the use of this theme which is whether old values were noble or shameful, and whether the young ones are truly liberated, or erasing history simply because it was inconvenient. The audience is swayed both ways, with relief that the old militarist order is gone, but with unease at how easily the new generation discards valuable history, and how Ono is discredited as a result and this generates pity. The effect of this is a challenge of the conventional, easily progressing narrative, as neither one side is determined to be correct to condemn or celebrate. The new world is freer but colder, and the audience is unsure whether Ono's situation is justice or just erasure of history in ignorance.

In addition, since the quote is spoken by another character and only absorbed by Ono, the audience is watching self-deception happen socially, not just in oneself, where there are two men helping each other to lie and not look, which is more unsettling to the audience than a singular narrator lying to himself, and reveals its deep roots even in society.

This shifts attention from the event to the psychology behind behind it.
